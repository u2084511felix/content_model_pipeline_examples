# Introduction: The Return of Schedule F as 'Schedule Policy/Career'

On January 20, 2025, President Donald Trump signed Executive Order 14171, marking the dramatic return and rebranding of the controversial Schedule F policy as "Schedule Policy/Career." This move, which immediately set off alarm bells among governance experts and federal employee unions, seeks to reclassify tens of thousands of federal civil servants in policy-related roles as at-will employees. By stripping these positions of long-standing civil service protections, the administration has opened the door to the mass replacement of experienced, nonpartisan professionals with political loyalists.

The reinstatement of Schedule F—now under the more innocuous-sounding label of "Schedule Policy/Career"—is not merely an administrative adjustment. It is a calculated effort to fundamentally alter the character and independence of the federal bureaucracy. This investigative report examines the origins, intent, and far-reaching implications of this policy, situating it within the broader context of Project 2025 and Agenda 47, and drawing on expert analysis, legal filings, and whistleblower testimony to expose the coordinated threat it poses to the foundations of U.S. democracy.

## A New Era of Executive Power

The executive order’s timing and scope signal a new era of presidential assertiveness over the federal workforce. By targeting policy-influencing roles, the administration aims to ensure that federal agencies are staffed with individuals who are not only competent but also loyal to the president’s agenda.

# What is Schedule Policy/Career? Origins, Intent, and Rebranding

Schedule F was first introduced in October 2020 by the Trump administration as an executive order (EO 13957) designed to reclassify certain federal employees in policy-making roles as excepted service, making them easier to hire and fire. The stated intent was to increase accountability and ensure that federal employees in influential positions were aligned with the administration’s priorities. However, critics immediately warned that the order would undermine the merit-based civil service system and open the door to politicization and patronage.

After being rescinded by President Biden in 2021, the policy has now returned in 2025 under the new name "Schedule Policy/Career" via Executive Order 14171. The rebranding is more than cosmetic: it is a strategic attempt to frame the policy as a neutral, career-focused reform, even as it revives the core mechanism of removing civil service protections from up to 50,000 policy-influencing positions. The Office of Personnel Management (OPM) has proposed rules to implement the change, and agencies have been directed to submit reclassification plans, setting the stage for a sweeping transformation of the federal workforce.

## From Schedule F to Schedule Policy/Career: What’s Changed?

While the name has changed, the underlying threat remains. The new order retains the power to reclassify and remove career civil servants, but with updated language to address some legal vulnerabilities identified in the original Schedule F.

# How the Policy Threatens the Independence of the Federal Civil Service

The core danger of Schedule Policy/Career lies in its capacity to erode the nonpartisan nature of the federal civil service. By making up to 50,000 policy-influencing positions subject to at-will employment, the administration can swiftly remove employees who are perceived as insufficiently loyal or who raise concerns about the legality or ethics of proposed policies. This undermines the principle of a merit-based, professional bureaucracy that serves the public interest rather than the political interests of the executive.

The mass reclassification and potential replacement of experienced civil servants with political appointees risks a loss of institutional memory, expertise, and continuity in government operations. It also creates a chilling effect, discouraging dissent and whistleblowing, and incentivizing compliance over competence. The result is a federal workforce that is less capable, less independent, and more vulnerable to political manipulation—conditions that have historically paved the way for abuses of power and democratic backsliding.

# Expert Analysis: Legal, Governance, and Union Perspectives

Legal scholars and good governance advocates have raised immediate and forceful objections to Schedule Policy/Career. They argue that the executive order violates the Civil Service Reform Act of 1978, which was enacted to prevent precisely this kind of politicization and to ensure that federal employment decisions are based on merit rather than political loyalty. Lawsuits filed by unions such as the National Treasury Employees Union (NTEU) and the American Federation of Government Employees (AFGE) contend that the order is an unconstitutional assertion of presidential authority and undermines the statutory protections that have long shielded the civil service from partisan interference.

Union leaders warn that the policy will have a devastating impact on morale, recruitment, and retention within the federal workforce. They point to the risk of a mass exodus of experienced professionals and the difficulty of attracting new talent to positions that no longer offer job security or protection from political retaliation. Governance experts, meanwhile, highlight the broader implications for democratic accountability, warning that the erosion of civil service independence is a classic precursor to executive overreach and the concentration of power.
